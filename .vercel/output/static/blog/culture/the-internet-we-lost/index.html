The Internet We Lost

On the old web, personal sites, webrings, and why I still think owning your corner of the internet matters.

#internet#tech-culture#indie-web#nostalgia

The Internet We Lost

I grew up on the internet before the timeline. Before the algorithm. Before engagement metrics and follower counts and the dopamine slot machine of infinite scroll.

I grew up on the web of GeoCities and forums. Of people who had personal sites that loaded with MIDI files and used <blink> tags unironically. Of webrings — literally webrings, where you’d click “next” and end up on some stranger’s page about their cat or their train set or their very specific opinion about a 1987 film.

It was weird. It was slow. Dial-up meant you’d listen to the modem negotiate a connection like two alien civilizations learning to talk to each other before a single pixel loaded.

It was also, in retrospect, remarkable.

What Changed

The social media platforms didn’t just capture attention — they captured identity. They gave us a profile instead of a site. A feed instead of a page. An algorithm instead of a blogroll.

The economics are obvious in hindsight: a centralized platform can harvest data, serve ads, and capture network effects in a way that a personal site cannot. So the gravity shifted.

But something was lost in that shift. Personal sites had a texture that profiles don’t. You could tell a lot about a person from how they designed their site — what they chose to show, how they organized it, what weird rabbit holes they put in the sidebar.

The Indie Web

There’s a small but persistent movement of people building their own corners of the web again. The IndieWeb community, personal blogs, newsletters, RSS feeds.

This site is my contribution to that. A business card and a blog, owned by me, hosted at my domain, styled however I want. No algorithm deciding who sees it. No engagement optimization. Just writing, put on the internet, in a place that I control.

If you’re a developer and you don’t have a personal site: make one. It doesn’t have to be fancy. It just has to be yours.
